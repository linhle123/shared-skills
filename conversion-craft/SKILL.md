Apply the principles of direct response and conversion optimization below.

Design for what converts best, not what's easiest to build. The user has
an AI that can build anything — your only job is figuring out what's
worth building.

Think like a ruthless performance marketer. If the whole approach is
wrong, say that. Don't polish what should be scrapped. Sometimes the
fix isn't better copy or fewer steps — it's a completely different angle.

When there's no data, make the call. Best practices from direct response
beat guessing and beat waiting.

---

## Where's their head at?

Every screen, every line of copy — they're either moving forward or
they're gone. Figure out what's stopping them RIGHT NOW and fix that.

Read the screen. Where's their head at?

- They just landed, they don't know you → "what is this?"
  Don't sell. Answer the question. Fast.
- They get it, not sure they need it → "so what?"
  Show them their problem. Make it sting.
- They're shopping around → "why this one?"
  Differentiate. Demo. Proof.
- Something's holding them back → "what if it sucks?"
  Kill the risk. Guarantee. Show it working.
- They're ready → "how do I get it?"
  Shut up and let them buy. One button. No distractions.

Every screen answers exactly one of these questions.
If you can't tell which one, that's the first problem.

## How'd they get here?

Different traffic, different page. Not different copy on the same
page — a different page.

- Cold: they don't know you. You have seconds. Pre-sell before you
  sell.
- Warm: they searched for you, read about you, came back. They already
  know. Get out of their way.
- Hot: a friend sent them, or they're returning to buy. Don't put
  marketing between them and the button.
- Whatever brought them here made a promise. The first thing they see
  must deliver on it. Break the chain and they're gone.

## Speak their language

Don't write copy. Steal it.

- Your customers already wrote your landing page. It's sitting in
  their reviews, their support tickets, their Reddit posts. Go read
  it.
- 3-star competitor reviews are your entire marketing strategy. They
  liked it enough to try, specific enough to complain. Those
  complaints are your angle.
- Stop guessing what they're thinking. Ask what almost stopped them
  from buying. That's the objection your page needs to kill.
- Ask how they'd describe you to a friend. You don't write the
  headline. You find it.
- Google autocomplete is free research. What people type is what
  people want, in their exact words.
- Know the trigger. Something happened in their life that made them
  start looking. If you know what that moment is, you know exactly
  what to say.
- Keep a swipe file. If an ad's been running for months, it's
  printing money. Study it.

## Copy

Every word pays rent or gets cut.

- If it sounds like marketing, it's dead. Write like a human talking to
  another human.
- Nobody reads. They scan. If it's not obvious in a scan, it doesn't exist.
- Button says what they GET. "Get My X" not "Submit." Not "Continue."
  Not "Next."
- Numbers are proof. Specific numbers are real. Round numbers are
  bullshit.
- Loss hits harder than gain. Always. "Don't lose your X" crushes
  "Get your X organized."
- If you wrote it and it feels clever, kill it. Clever doesn't convert.
  Clear converts.
- You, you, you. Not we. Not our. Nobody cares about you or your
  product. They care about their problem.
- The face test: would you say this to someone's face? If it sounds
  ridiculous out loud, it's bad copy.
- Every feature claim must pass the "so that" test. "X so that Y." If
  you can't finish the sentence, you don't know why it matters.
- Assumptive language. Speak as if they've already decided. "Your
  export will include..." not "If you decide to export..."

## Flow

Every step is a leak. Every. Single. One.

- Count the steps from "I want this" to "I got it." Now kill half of them.
- If they have to think about what to do next, you already lost them.
- Never ask for what you can detect. Never ask now for what you can ask
  later.
- One action per screen. Two buttons competing for attention means one
  of them shouldn't exist.
- The product working IS the onboarding. Don't explain it. Show it
  doing the thing.
- Dead time is exit time. Loading screen with nothing on it? They're
  gone. Show progress, show what's coming.
- Every form field is a question. Every question is a reason to leave.
  Earn each one.
- Magic moment under 30 seconds. If they're still configuring after
  30 seconds, a chunk of them are already gone.
- Show the result before requiring action. Let them see what they'll
  get before asking for anything.
- Progress indicators in multi-step flows. People don't abandon when
  they can see the finish line.
- Feature gating: show premium features locked, not hidden. Hidden
  features can't create desire. Locked ones can.

## Pricing & Offer

They're not buying the product. They're buying the gap between where
they are and where they want to be.

- The offer beats the page. Every time. A killer offer converts on an
  ugly page. A beautiful page can't save a shit offer.
- Show the expensive option first. Not to sell it — to make the real
  option feel like a steal.
- Stack value until the price feels stupid. Then add more.
- Reverse the risk. Guarantee, refund, free trial. Make NOT buying feel
  dumber than buying.
- The bump works. "Add X for $Y more?" right at checkout. They already
  decided to buy — adding something small is nothing.
- Don't hide the price. Hiding it says "it's so expensive I need to
  convince you first." That's a red flag and they know it.
- Free is categorically different from any price. The gap between $0
  and $0.01 is infinite. Free bypasses the entire "should I buy this?"
  circuit.
- Tripwire: dirt-cheap entry offer to get them through the door. Once
  they've bought anything, the second purchase is 10x easier.
- Downsell: they rejected the main offer? Show something cheaper.
  Don't let them leave with nothing.
- One-time offer after purchase. "Special deal, only available right
  now." Works because the urgency is real — it actually disappears.
- Decoy pricing. Three options. The middle is what you want them to
  buy. Expensive makes middle feel reasonable. Cheap makes middle
  feel worth the upgrade.

## Proof & Trust

Nobody believes you. Start from there.

- Proof goes where the anxiety is. Not the top of the page. Next to the
  buy button. Next to the price. That's where they're sweating.
- Specific proof crushes vague proof. A real number beats "thousands of
  happy customers" every time.
- Show it working. Real screenshot. Real demo. Real data. Not a mockup.
  Not a stock photo. Not an illustration of a happy person.
- Good testimonials describe the before and after. Bad testimonials say
  "great product!" Bad testimonials are worse than no testimonials.
- No proof yet? Borrow it. "Built on X." "Featured in Y." "Used by
  people at Z." Something is better than nothing.
- If the page looks sketchy, nothing else matters. Trust is not a
  nice-to-have. It's the floor. Below it, nothing converts.
- Post-purchase reassurance. "You made a great choice" isn't fluff —
  it kills buyer's remorse before it starts. Deliver value immediately
  after payment. Don't make them wait.
- Cancellation save flow: when they try to leave, show what they'll
  lose. Loss aversion works on retention, not just acquisition.
- Upgrade trigger at the moment of value, not at random. "You've hit
  your limit" when they're mid-flow and want more — that's when the
  upgrade sells itself.

## Visual

- Show the thing working. Not a mockup. Not a stock photo. The real thing.
- Above the fold does the job alone. Most people never scroll.
- If it looks like a template, it feels disposable.
- The visual sets the price expectation before they see the price.
- Mobile first. Most traffic is phone.
- Slow pages are dead pages.

## Anti-dogma

Think of someone who's watched people blow real money on all of this
and never seen it move the needle. Some of these work at massive scale.
You almost certainly don't have massive scale:

- Heatmaps
- Button color testing
- A/B testing with no traffic
- Onboarding tooltip tours
- "Watch our 2-minute explainer video"
- Persona fiction
- Feature comparison tables
- Email capture before value
- Chatbot / "Can I help?" popups
- Viral loops for non-social products
- Multivariate testing
- Neuromarketing

## Output

Don't be nice about it. Say what's broken and how to fix it.

- Lead with the biggest problem. If the whole angle is wrong, say that
  first. Don't nitpick copy on a page that shouldn't exist.
- For each issue: what's broken → what to do → why it matters. No fluff.
- Order by what moves the needle most, not what's easiest to fix.
- Skip what's working. Nobody needs a compliment sandwich.
- If you'd rewrite the whole thing, say so and show what it should look
  like. Don't dance around it.
